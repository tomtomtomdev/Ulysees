# Trades About to Happen — Weis Framework

Weis's modern application of Wyckoff: bar-by-bar reading of price and volume, with wave analysis to quantify effort vs. result, and pattern recognition for setups that are about to resolve.

## Step 1: Bar reading fundamentals

Every bar carries information in four dimensions. For the recent bars (typically last 5–15), read:

- **Range (spread)** — wide or narrow relative to recent bars
- **Close position** — top third, middle, bottom third of the bar's range
- **Volume** — heavy, average, or light relative to recent bars
- **Context** — where the bar sits in the larger structure (range, trend, breakout, retest)

Combinations:
- **Wide-spread up bar, close on high, heavy volume** — demand winning, especially after a low
- **Wide-spread down bar, close on low, heavy volume** — supply winning, especially after a high
- **Narrow-spread bar, low volume, near recent low** — no supply (bullish signal)
- **Narrow-spread bar, low volume, near recent high** — no demand (bearish signal)
- **Wide-spread bar, heavy volume, close in middle** — absorption (opposing flow is stepping in)

## Step 2: Effort vs. result

Weis's central principle. Identify bars where volume (effort) doesn't match price progress (result):

- **High effort, low result up** — supply absorbing buying; bearish if it appears at potential top
- **High effort, low result down** — demand absorbing selling; bullish if it appears at potential bottom
- **Low effort, low result** — neutral; tells you the current phase is quiet
- **Low effort, high result** — exhaustion of the prior trend's opposition; trend will likely continue once tested

The diagnostic moments are when effort and result diverge.

## Step 3: Weis wave analysis

Weis added a quantitative tool — wave analysis — to the Wyckoff toolkit:

- A wave is a continuous price move in one direction; reverses when price moves against the wave by more than a defined amount
- For each wave, sum the volume traded during that wave
- Compare wave volume to prior waves in the same direction

Signals:
- **Wave volume declining on advances** in an uptrend = weakening demand
- **Wave volume rising on declines** in an uptrend = supply increasing; warning
- **Wave volume declining on declines** in a downtrend = supply drying up; potential bottom
- **Wave volume rising on advances** during a base = demand returning; potential bottom

## Step 4: Identify the setup type

Weis is most useful for setups that are about to resolve. Common setups:

**Spring / shakeout** (bullish)
- Price breaks below a support level
- Volume is heavy OR notably light (each tells a different story)
- Price returns above support quickly
- The next 1–3 bars determine the validity: low-volume retest, wide-spread up bar = clean setup

**Upthrust** (bearish)
- Price breaks above resistance
- Volume heavy or notably light
- Price returns below resistance
- Follow-through: weak rally on light volume = clean short setup

**No-supply test**
- After accumulation, a pullback on narrow spread and light volume into prior demand
- The lack of supply confirms accumulation; the next up bar is often the setup trigger

**No-demand top**
- After distribution or extended advance, an attempt to make a new high on narrow spread and light volume
- Lack of demand confirms distribution; the next down bar is the trigger

**Sign of strength / weakness**
- Wide-spread, high-volume bar in the direction of the larger structure
- Confirms phase transition (Phase C to D in Wyckoff terms)

## Step 5: Context — where in the structure

A no-supply bar in the middle of a downtrend is meaningless. The same bar at a likely accumulation low is high-conviction. Always frame the bar in the broader structure:
- Trend or range?
- Near major support or resistance?
- After a significant prior signal (climax, automatic rally/reaction)?
- Aligned with higher-timeframe direction?

## Step 6: Trigger and confirmation

Weis trade structure:
- **Trigger bar** — the bar that signals the setup is resolving (e.g., wide-spread up bar after no-supply test)
- **Entry** — typically at or just above the trigger bar's close, or on the next bar's open
- **Stop** — below the spring low / above the upthrust high; tight relative to the setup
- **Target** — based on the size of the prior cause (range height, prior wave size)

## Step 7: What can go wrong

- A "spring" that fails (volume too heavy, no follow-through) is a major bearish signal — the long setup inverts
- An "upthrust" that doesn't fade quickly may be a real breakout
- Low-volume bars in low-volume markets carry less information; context is everything
- Single-bar signals are weaker than confluence of multiple bars over several days

## Output format

1. **Setup identification** — which Weis pattern is present, or none.
2. **Bar-by-bar reading** — the last 5–10 bars with range/close/volume and what each says.
3. **Effort vs. result diagnosis** — where the divergences are.
4. **Wave analysis** — recent wave volumes and what they imply.
5. **Context** — where in the larger structure this sits.
6. **Trigger and trade construction** — entry, stop, target, with bar-based rationale.
7. **Failure conditions** — what would tell you the setup has invalidated.

## What to avoid

- Don't read single bars in isolation. Context is the whole game.
- Don't trade no-supply or no-demand setups without confirmation. The next bar matters as much as the signal bar.
- Don't apply this to illiquid stocks. Volume signals require real volume.
- Don't ignore the higher timeframe. A bullish setup on the 5-minute against a daily downtrend is a fade in waiting.
- Don't size large on tape-reading alone. This is a precision tool, not a license for conviction.
