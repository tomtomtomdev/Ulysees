# Little Book of Value Investing — Browne Framework

Browne's book distills the Tweedy Browne approach: cheap by simple metrics, financially safe, and ideally with quiet positive signals from insiders. Use for a brisk but disciplined value assessment.

## Step 1: Quick cheapness screen

Compute and judge each:

- **P/E ratio** — use trailing earnings; lower is better; below market average is the threshold of interest.
- **Price-to-book** — below 1× is a strong signal; below 1.5× is interesting.
- **Price-to-cash-flow** — operating cash flow yield; demands a real cushion vs. bond yields.
- **EV / EBIT** — captures debt; useful when capital structures differ.
- **Dividend yield** — sustained dividend is a quality marker.

A stock should be cheap on most of these, not just one. Single-metric cheapness is usually a trap.

## Step 2: Owner earnings

Earnings can be distorted by accounting choices. Browne emphasizes cash a true owner could extract:

**Owner earnings = Net income + D&A − maintenance capex − working capital needs**

Compute and compare to market cap. An owner-earnings yield above bond yields with growth potential is the basic case.

## Step 3: Financial safety

A cheap stock is only useful if the company survives. Check:
- Long-term debt manageable relative to equity and earnings
- Interest coverage well above 1
- Current ratio adequate for the industry
- No looming covenant or maturity issues

If safety fails, the cheapness might be deserved.

## Step 4: Insider activity

Browne weights insider buying heavily. It's a noisy but useful signal because insiders buy for only one reason — they expect the stock to go up.

- **Open-market purchases** by multiple insiders, especially CEO/CFO — a positive signal.
- **Cluster buying** (several insiders within weeks) — stronger.
- **Insider selling** — much weaker as a negative signal (lots of innocent reasons), but heavy concentrated selling is worth noting.

## Step 5: International and small-cap awareness

Browne argues value works globally and is often easier in less-followed markets. Note:
- Is this a domestic-only check, or should comparable international peers be considered?
- Is the company small or under-covered in a way that creates inefficiency?

## Step 6: Patience and the time horizon

Browne is explicit: value investing requires patience measured in years. Note expected holding period and what would prompt a sale (price approaches value, fundamentals deteriorate, better opportunity appears).

## Output format

1. **Verdict** — cheap and safe / cheap but risky / not cheap / cheap for a reason (value trap).
2. **Cheapness scorecard** — table of the metrics with values.
3. **Owner earnings yield** — number and comparison to bond yield.
4. **Safety check** — pass/fail with key figures.
5. **Insider activity** — recent buys/sells, weight given.
6. **Notable risks** — 2–3 bullets.
7. **What to watch for** — what would change the verdict.

## What to avoid

- Don't get excited about one cheap metric while ignoring others.
- Don't ignore the balance sheet because the income statement is exciting.
- Don't weight insider selling as heavily as insider buying.
- Don't recommend without acknowledging that value can take years to work.
