# Mind Over Markets — Dalton Framework

Dalton's framework applies J. Peter Steidlmayer's Market Profile and auction theory to market analysis. The thesis: markets are continuous two-way auctions seeking value, and price/time/volume structure reveals where participants find value, where they don't, and what their intent is.

## Step 1: Auction theory foundation

Markets are auctions. They serve one purpose: facilitating trade. They move higher to find sellers and lower to find buyers, always seeking the price where the most volume transacts (point of control).

Key concepts:
- **Price** — what you pay
- **Value** — where most trade occurs over time (volume- and time-weighted)
- **Price away from value** — markets advertise prices to attract opposing flow
- **Acceptance vs. rejection** — when price stays at a level over time, it's accepted; when it spends little time, it's rejected

The Market Profile is the visualization of this process.

## Step 2: Read the Market Profile

For the period in question (day, week, longer composite), identify:

- **Point of Control (POC)** — price with the most TPOs (time-price opportunities); the fairest price
- **Value Area (VA)** — typically the 70% of TPOs around the POC; the range where price was accepted
- **Value Area High (VAH) and Low (VAL)** — boundaries of value
- **Range extremes** — the day's or period's high and low; often rejection points
- **Profile shape** — normal (bell-curve), trending (elongated, P-shape or b-shape), neutral (multiple distributions), double-distribution (two value areas)

The shape tells you the type of day or period:
- **Normal day** — opens within prior value, range develops around POC, balanced auction
- **Normal variation** — wider range but balanced overall
- **Trend day** — one-directional, narrow value area at one extreme, initiative activity dominates
- **Double-distribution day** — two separate value areas form; transitional market
- **Neutral day** — opens near middle, tests both extremes, closes near middle

## Step 3: Initiative vs. responsive activity

Dalton's central distinction:

- **Initiative activity** — participants buying above value or selling below value; expressing conviction that value will move in their direction. Bullish initiative = buying above VAH. Bearish initiative = selling below VAL.
- **Responsive activity** — participants buying below value or selling above value; expressing the view that price is away from fair and will return. Responsive selling at VAH; responsive buying at VAL.

In a healthy trend, initiative activity dominates. In balance, responsive activity dominates. Diagnose which is operating.

## Step 4: Balance vs. imbalance

Markets oscillate between balance (range, two-sided trade) and imbalance (trend, one-sided trade):

- **Balance** — value area overlaps with prior periods; POC stable; trade is responsive at extremes
- **Imbalance** — value area shifts directionally period over period; initiative activity expanding range; trend developing
- **Breakout from balance** — initiative activity drives price out of the prior value area; if it sustains, a new auction direction is established

Identify which regime the market is in. Trade construction differs sharply between regimes.

## Step 5: Reference levels for decision-making

Key levels to mark on any chart:

- **Prior day's VAH, POC, VAL** — most relevant near-term references
- **Prior week's VAH, POC, VAL** — bigger structure
- **Composite POC** — over a longer period; major reference point
- **Naked POCs** — prior POCs that haven't been retested; often act as magnets
- **Single prints / unfilled gaps** — areas of fast price movement with little time spent; often retested

## Step 6: Open and opening type

The first 30–60 minutes set the day's character:

- **Open-Drive** — opens and moves directionally without retest; strongest signal; trend day likely
- **Open-Test-Drive** — opens, tests the other side briefly, then drives; trend with confirmation
- **Open-Rejection-Reverse** — opens, moves one way, reverses; suggests the open was wrong
- **Open-Auction** — opens within prior value, two-sided; balance day likely

The opening type informs day-type expectation and trade approach.

## Step 7: Long timeframe vs. short timeframe traders

Dalton distinguishes:
- **Long timeframe (LTF)** — institutional, hedgers, value players; trade infrequently but in size; move markets when they act
- **Short timeframe (STF)** — locals, day traders, scalpers; provide liquidity, fade extremes

Initiative activity from LTF is the most powerful directional force. Look for evidence of LTF involvement: range expansion, sustained moves out of value, persistent one-sided trade.

## Step 8: Trade construction by market type

- **Balance market** — fade extremes (sell VAH, buy VAL); responsive trading; tight stops just beyond extremes; targets at POC
- **Imbalance / trend market** — buy pullbacks to value in uptrend; sell rallies to value in downtrend; wider stops; targets at prior major levels
- **Breakout from balance** — enter on confirmed acceptance outside prior VA; stop back inside; target measured from balance height

Match the trade structure to the market type. The most common mistake is fading a trend or chasing balance.

## Output format

1. **Profile snapshot** — POC, VAH, VAL, range, shape.
2. **Day or period type** — normal, normal variation, trend, neutral, double-distribution.
3. **Initiative vs. responsive read** — which is operating and where.
4. **Balance vs. imbalance** — current regime; possible transition signs.
5. **Reference levels** — prior day VA, naked POCs, single prints, key composite levels.
6. **Opening type** — if intraday, what the open implied.
7. **LTF activity inference** — institutional involvement or absence.
8. **Trade construction** — entry zone, stop, target, sized to market type.
9. **What would invalidate** — failure conditions.

## What to avoid

- Don't apply Market Profile to illiquid or low-volume instruments. Profile structure needs density to be meaningful.
- Don't trade balance and imbalance the same way. The most common loss source.
- Don't ignore the open. Opening type filters the day type early.
- Don't chase initiative activity late in the move. Best entries are early in the auction direction.
- Don't fade trend days. They run further than fader instincts expect.
