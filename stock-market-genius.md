# Stock Market Genius — Greenblatt Special Situations Framework

Greenblatt's thesis: corporate events create forced, mechanical, non-economic trading that produces persistent mispricings for analysts willing to do the work. Apply this framework to any corporate event.

## Step 1: Identify the situation type

Each type has its own dynamics:
- **Spin-off** — parent distributes shares of subsidiary to shareholders.
- **Merger arbitrage** — buy target, sometimes short acquirer, capture deal spread.
- **Bankruptcy / restructuring** — buy claims or post-emergence equity.
- **Recapitalization** — leveraged recap, share buyback, dividend recap.
- **Stub stock** — parent after major asset sale or spin-off.
- **Rights offering** — discounted share issue, often with oversubscription privilege.
- **Risk arbitrage variations** — partial spin-offs, tracking stocks, equity carve-outs.

## Step 2: Why is mispricing likely?

The whole point is to identify the structural seller or buyer who acts for non-economic reasons. Examples:

- **Spin-off**: index funds and large institutions receive shares of a smaller company they can't or won't hold and dump indiscriminately. The smaller the spin-off relative to the parent, the heavier the forced selling.
- **Merger**: deal spreads reflect a probability-weighted risk of break that's often overstated for friendly deals.
- **Post-bankruptcy**: original holders are wiped out, new equity goes to creditors who often want cash, not equity in the reorganized firm.
- **Rights offering**: dilution fear leads to selling; rights themselves are often mispriced.

State the specific reason mispricing is likely in this case. If you can't, the edge isn't real.

## Step 3: Insider alignment

Greenblatt weighs insider behavior heavily in special situations. Look for:
- Insiders taking shares, options, or warrants in the new/post-event entity
- Management financially incentivized in the smaller spin-off, not the parent
- Significant insider buying after the event

A spin-off where management goes with the spin and gets large equity grants is materially more interesting than one where they stay with the parent.

## Step 4: Valuation of the post-event entity

Pretend the event has happened and the new security exists as a standalone. Apply normal value analysis:
- What are the assets and earnings power?
- What is the appropriate multiple?
- What does the balance sheet look like post-event?
- Compare to peers as a standalone, not as part of the prior conglomerate.

For spin-offs especially, the smaller, less-followed entity is often where the value hides.

## Step 5: Catalyst and timeline

Special situations have built-in catalysts. Map them:
- Event date (effective date of spin, merger close, plan confirmation)
- Lock-up expirations
- Index inclusion dates
- Earnings reports after the event when the standalone story emerges
- Sell-side coverage initiation

The catalyst's predictability is part of the edge.

## Step 6: Risks specific to the situation

Each event type has signature risks:
- **Spin-offs**: leverage dumped onto the spin, contingent liabilities allocated to the spin, sub-scale operations.
- **Merger arb**: deal break (antitrust, financing, due diligence, MAC clause), spread compression.
- **Bankruptcy**: fraudulent conveyance, classification disputes, valuation fights, tax issues with NOL.
- **Rights offerings**: dilution if you don't subscribe, illiquidity in rights themselves.

## Step 7: Position structure

Many of these situations support more sophisticated structures:
- Long spin, short parent (or vice versa) to isolate the spin's value
- Long target, short acquirer for stock deals
- Buy debt rather than equity in distressed names
- Use options when the catalyst date is known

Mention if the situation supports a structure beyond plain long equity.

## Output format

1. **Situation type** and **expected event date**.
2. **Why mispricing exists** — the specific forced seller/buyer story.
3. **Insider alignment** — what the people closest to it are doing.
4. **Post-event valuation** — value of the new entity as a standalone.
5. **Catalysts** — sequence of value-realizing events.
6. **Risks** — signature risks of this event type plus deal-specific.
7. **Trade structure** — straight long, paired, debt vs. equity, options.
8. **Verdict** — fat pitch, marginal, or pass.

## What to avoid

- Don't treat a spin-off as automatically attractive. Many are dumping grounds.
- Don't trade merger arb without checking the spread vs. the realistic break probability.
- Don't ignore the parent in a spin analysis — sometimes the parent is the better trade.
- Don't assume insider alignment without checking the proxy or 8-K filings.
- Don't size special situations like core long-term holdings; the catalyst nature changes the sizing logic.
