# Poor Charlie's Almanack — Munger Framework

Munger's central method: build a lattice of mental models from multiple disciplines and check each idea against all of them. Apply that approach to investment analysis, with special attention to inversion and the psychological forces that cause misjudgment.

## Step 1: Inversion — start by trying to lose

Munger's signature move: ask not "how do I win" but "how do I lose," and then avoid that. For this investment, list explicitly:

- **Top 5 ways this investment fails permanently**, each as concrete as possible.
- **Top 5 mistakes a typical investor would make analyzing this**.
- **What would Charlie Munger be most afraid of here?**

If any of these failure modes is plausible and unpriced, that's the dispositive issue.

## Step 2: Apply the lattice of mental models

Pass the situation through models from multiple disciplines:

**Microeconomics**
- What's the moat? Brand, scale, switching costs, network effects, low-cost production, regulatory.
- Pricing power — can it raise prices without losing volume?
- Returns on incremental capital — high and sustainable, or commoditizing?
- Competitive destruction — is technology or business model change eroding the moat?

**Psychology of misjudgment** (Munger's 25 standard causes — pick the relevant ones)
- Are investors anchored to recent prices or narrative?
- Is social proof driving the consensus?
- Is incentive-caused bias distorting management or analyst behavior?
- Is availability bias (recent vivid events) shaping the multiple?
- Is the company in a "lollapalooza" situation where multiple biases compound?

**Game theory**
- What does the rational competitor do in response to this company's moves?
- Capacity additions, price cuts, response from incumbents — are they being modeled?

**Biology / ecology**
- Does the industry behave like an ecosystem with co-evolving species?
- Is the company an invasive species (high growth in a new niche) or a stressed incumbent?

**Physics / thermodynamics**
- Is the business compounding (positive feedback) or in decay (entropy without renewal)?

**Mathematics / statistics**
- Base rates — what fraction of comparable companies historically achieve what's implied by the price?
- Compounding math — is the implied growth rate sustainable given the size of the company?

Note which models illuminate the situation; don't force all of them.

## Step 3: "Great business at a fair price"

Munger pushed Buffett from "fair business at a great price" toward "great business at a fair price." Assess:

- **Quality of the business** — high returns on capital, reinvestment runway, durable moat.
- **Fair price** — not cheap on Graham metrics necessarily, but reasonable for the quality.
- **Compounding logic** — over a long hold, business quality matters more than entry multiple.

If the business is mediocre, this framework is the wrong one — Munger would tell you to skip it.

## Step 4: Circle of competence

Munger insists on knowing the edge of what you understand:
- What do you actually understand about this business?
- What are you taking on faith?
- Are the things you're taking on faith dispositive?

Be honest. Outside the circle of competence, the right move is pass, not stretch.

## Step 5: Incentives

Munger: "Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome." Examine:
- **Management incentives** — compensation tied to what (revenue, EPS, ROIC, stock price)? Does that align with shareholders?
- **Industry incentives** — what behavior does the industry's reward structure encourage?
- **Analyst incentives** — what's the sell-side really being paid to do?
- **Your own incentives** — why are you looking at this? Bias check.

## Step 6: Avoid the standard stupidities

Munger's negative wisdom: most progress comes from not being stupid, not from being brilliant. Check:
- Not overpaying for growth
- Not buying because everyone else is
- Not anchoring to purchase price or recent high
- Not confusing activity with progress
- Not betting the farm on any single idea
- Not following pundits, gurus, or the financial media

State which of these is the most relevant trap for this specific situation.

## Output format

1. **Inversion list** — top failure modes for this investment.
2. **Mental model pass** — which models apply and what they say.
3. **Business quality** — moat, returns on capital, compounding character.
4. **Fair price assessment** — not a precise valuation, a judgment of whether price is reasonable for quality.
5. **Circle of competence** — what you understand vs. what you're taking on faith.
6. **Incentive map** — management, industry, analyst, your own.
7. **Standard stupidity to avoid** — the specific trap for this situation.
8. **Munger-style verdict** — buy / pass / too hard.

## What to avoid

- Don't skip inversion. It's the most important step and the most often skipped.
- Don't pretend competence outside your circle. "Too hard" is a legitimate and frequent verdict.
- Don't apply all 80+ mental models mechanically; pick the ones that illuminate this case.
- Don't ignore management's incentives because the business is good.
- Don't confuse Munger's quality bias with paying any price for quality.
