# Reminiscences of a Stock Operator — Livermore Framework

Lefèvre's book captures Jesse Livermore's approach: speculation as a serious profession, requiring tape reading, patience, position discipline, and psychological control. Apply the framework as a trader's lens — different in goal from value investing, but complementary as a perspective on what's actually happening in the stock.

## Step 1: Determine the trend and the line of least resistance

Livermore's central concept: stocks move along a line of least resistance, established by the balance of supply and demand. The trader's job is to identify that line and not fight it.

- **Major trend** — multi-month/year direction; the most powerful force
- **Intermediate trend** — multi-week/month moves within the major
- **Minor trend** — daily noise; mostly ignored

For the stock in question, determine:
- What's the major trend?
- What's the current intermediate posture (with major, counter to major, transitioning)?
- Where is the line of least resistance pointing right now?

Trade only with the line of least resistance. If the line is unclear or transitional, the right action is to stand aside.

## Step 2: Pivotal points

Livermore traded specific decision points where the supply/demand balance demonstrably shifts:

- **Continuation pivot** — pullback to support in an established trend; clean test that holds confirms the trend's continuation
- **Reversal pivot** — break of a multi-month base or top; the moment when the line of least resistance shifts
- **Breakout pivot** — clean breakout from a long sideways range on confirming volume; the move out of accumulation

Identify whether the current chart is at, approaching, or far from a pivotal point. Trades are entered at pivots, not in the middle.

## Step 3: Probe and pyramid

Livermore's position-building method:

- **Probe small first** — open with a fraction of intended position to test the thesis
- **Add only if proven right** — pyramid into winners, never into losers
- **Each add at a pre-defined trigger** — usually a higher pivot or continuation pattern
- **Total position size limited by conviction and risk tolerance**

The discipline: average up, never down. The crowd's instinct is to average down; Livermore's lesson is that averaging down feeds capital into a losing thesis.

## Step 4: Cut losses quickly

The single most repeated lesson in the book. Specifically:
- A predetermined stop-loss level, set before entry
- Honored without exception; no "give it one more day"
- A loss of 7–10% is the typical Livermore-tradition threshold (he used different rules in different eras)
- Once stopped, walk away from the trade and reassess later

## Step 5: Sit through the trend

The other most repeated lesson, harder than cutting losses:
- "It was never my thinking that made the big money for me. It always was my sitting."
- Once a position is established in a confirmed trend, the impulse to take small profits is the enemy of large profits
- Hold until the line of least resistance shifts or a major pivot fails

The tension between cutting losses (acting fast on wrongness) and sitting on winners (acting slowly on rightness) is the core psychological discipline.

## Step 6: Tape reading

For Livermore, the tape revealed what no fundamental analysis could:

- **Volume confirmation or denial** — moves on heavy volume are real; moves on light volume are suspect
- **Behavior at resistance and support** — easy break, struggle and pause, or rejection
- **Response to news** — strong stock shrugs off bad news; weak stock fails to respond to good news
- **Sector and tape alignment** — leaders in leading sectors; laggards are usually laggards for a reason

Apply: what does the tape say about this stock? Confirm or invalidate any thesis you bring from outside.

## Step 7: Psychology

The book's deepest material. Address:

- **Hope, fear, greed, ignorance** — Livermore named these as the four enemies
- **Self-control** — most losses come from breaking your own rules, not from market action
- **Patience** — the right pitch comes infrequently; the cost of trading the wrong pitch is high
- **Independence** — the tape, not tipsters, friends, or the news, is the source of truth
- **Conviction without stubbornness** — strong belief in the thesis, but instant abandonment when proven wrong

State the most relevant psychological trap for this specific setup.

## Step 8: Position context — speculation, not investment

Livermore was a speculator. Be honest about what this framework is and isn't:
- It's about catching trends, not owning businesses
- Holding periods are weeks to months, not years
- The thesis is technical/flow-based, not value-based
- Drawdowns are accepted as the cost of doing business; positions are not "long-term holds" by default

A Livermore analysis should be marked as such, not blended into a value framework.

## Output format

1. **Major trend and line of least resistance** — direction, strength, clarity.
2. **Pivotal point status** — at a pivot, approaching one, between pivots.
3. **Entry plan** — initial probe size, scale-up triggers, total position cap.
4. **Stop discipline** — exact level, basis, what triggers exit.
5. **Holding plan** — what signals justify sitting through pullbacks; what signals justify exit.
6. **Tape diagnostics** — volume, news response, sector alignment.
7. **Psychological note** — most relevant trap for this setup.
8. **Verdict** — actionable speculation, marginal, or stand aside.

## What to avoid

- Don't apply Livermore to long-term value investing decisions. Different framework, different horizons.
- Don't ignore the cut-losses rule. It's not a suggestion.
- Don't average down. The instinct is universal; the lesson is universal.
- Don't trade between pivots. Most losses come from impatience between setups.
- Don't act on tips. Livermore is unambiguous: the tape is the source of truth.
