# Volume Price Analysis — Coulling Framework

VPA is the disciplined reading of price action validated (or invalidated) by volume. The core thesis: volume is the only objective measure of activity, and combining it with price tells you whether moves are genuine or manufactured.

## Step 1: Volume baselines

Before reading individual bars, establish baselines:
- **Average volume** over the recent period (20–50 bars)
- **Volume categories**: low (well below average), average, high (clearly above), ultra-high (climactic, 2×+ average)
- **Volume in context of the chart's regime** — accumulation ranges have characteristically declining volume; trends have volume that confirms direction

## Step 2: The four bar combinations

Every bar is read as a price action + volume pairing. Four combinations and their meanings:

**Rising price + rising volume** — bullish confirmation. Buying pressure is real. Trend support.

**Rising price + falling volume** — bearish divergence. The advance lacks participation; supply may be quietly absorbing demand. Watch for reversal.

**Falling price + rising volume** — bearish confirmation. Selling pressure is real. Trend support for downtrend.

**Falling price + falling volume** — bullish divergence (in a downtrend). Selling is exhausting. Watch for accumulation.

State which combination dominates the current 5–10 bars.

## Step 3: Spread + close + volume — the three-factor read

Each individual bar carries information in three dimensions:

- **Spread (range)** — wide, average, narrow
- **Close position** — top, middle, bottom of the bar
- **Volume** — relative to baseline

Diagnostic patterns:

- **Wide spread up, close on high, high volume** — strong demand, genuine bullish bar
- **Wide spread up, close in middle, ultra-high volume** — buying climax / absorption; supply stepped in at the high
- **Narrow spread up, close in middle, ultra-high volume** — heavy supply meeting demand; warning
- **Wide spread down, close on low, high volume** — strong supply, genuine bearish bar
- **Wide spread down, close in middle, ultra-high volume** — selling climax / absorption; demand stepped in
- **Narrow spread, low volume, near support** — no supply (bullish if accumulation is the context)
- **Narrow spread, low volume, near resistance** — no demand (bearish if distribution is the context)

## Step 4: Climactic volume

Climactic bars are the highest-information events:

- **Selling climax** — vertical drop, wide-spread bar, ultra-high volume, close usually off the low with a tail. Marks potential bottom — but only if confirmed by subsequent action (automatic rally, secondary test holding).
- **Buying climax** — vertical advance, wide-spread bar, ultra-high volume, close usually off the high with a tail. Marks potential top — confirmed by subsequent automatic reaction and failed retest.
- **Stopping volume** — appears in a trend; ultra-high volume with narrow spread and close opposite the trend direction. Signals exhaustion of the trend.

Climactic volume alone doesn't reverse a trend — confirmation comes from what happens next.

## Step 5: Tests and retests

Markets confirm or reject signals through tests. VPA gives tests special attention:

- **Test of a low after a selling climax** — should be on lower volume than the climax. Heavy volume on retest = supply still present, the low may break. Light volume on retest = supply has dried up, the low likely holds.
- **Test of a high after a buying climax** — should be on lower volume than the climax. Heavy volume = demand still present, possible breakout; light volume = no follow-through, top is likely in.
- **Pullback in a trend** — should be on lower volume than the prior advance. Volume rising during pullback = trend in trouble.

## Step 6: Divergence between price and volume

The most actionable VPA signals are divergences:
- New high in price with lower volume than the previous high = weak high, likely to fail
- New low in price with lower volume than the previous low = weak low, likely to hold
- Sustained advance with declining volume = lack of conviction; trend at risk

Divergences are early warnings, not immediate triggers.

## Step 7: Smart money inference

VPA's purpose is to read where the informed players are positioned:
- Heavy volume at significant prices (climaxes, tests, breakouts) is the footprint
- Quiet accumulation in a range shows up as declining volume punctuated by occasional heavy buying bars
- Distribution shows up as wide-spread up bars on heavy volume that fail to make significant progress (absorption at the top)

Frame the analysis in terms of what smart money is most likely doing.

## Step 8: Multi-timeframe alignment

VPA gains conviction from alignment:
- Daily-chart climax confirmed by weekly-chart climax = stronger signal
- Intraday VPA setup against daily trend = less reliable than aligned
- Volume patterns on higher timeframes have more weight

## Output format

1. **Volume baseline** — what counts as low, average, high, ultra-high for this chart.
2. **Dominant price+volume combination** — across the recent 5–10 bars.
3. **Key bar diagnostics** — the most informative bars and what they show.
4. **Climactic events** — recent buying or selling climaxes, with the confirmation status.
5. **Test history** — retests and what their volume revealed.
6. **Divergences** — present or absent.
7. **Smart money inference** — what the institutional/informed players are likely doing.
8. **Verdict and trade frame** — bullish/bearish/neutral, with entry, stop, target where applicable.

## What to avoid

- Don't read volume without spread and close context. Volume alone is incomplete.
- Don't trade climactic bars without confirmation. Climaxes mark potential turning points, not certainties.
- Don't apply VPA to illiquid stocks; the volume signal degrades.
- Don't ignore divergences just because the trend looks strong. They're warnings.
- Don't use VPA as a complete framework. It's a powerful confirmation tool but should be paired with structure (Wyckoff, support/resistance, fundamentals where relevant).
