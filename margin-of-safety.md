# Margin of Safety — Klarman Framework

Klarman's central conviction: investors should think first about avoiding loss, second about making money. Apply this framework when the situation calls for risk-first, bottom-up, contrarian analysis.

## Step 1: Identify the type of opportunity

Klarman invests across a wider opportunity set than classic Graham. Classify:
- **Undervalued security** — straightforward Graham/Buffett-style mispricing.
- **Special situation** — spin-off, merger arb, restructuring, rights offering.
- **Distressed debt / bankruptcy** — claim on the assets at a fraction of face.
- **Liquidation** — explicit wind-down with known cash distribution.
- **Complex / orphaned situation** — anything Wall Street is structurally unwilling or unable to analyze.

The opportunity type dictates how to think about value and catalyst.

## Step 2: Bottom-up valuation by multiple methods

Klarman rejects single-method valuation. Apply at least two of:

1. **Net present value of free cash flows** — conservative assumptions, discount rate that reflects real risk, scrutinize terminal value.
2. **Liquidation value** — what would creditors and shareholders receive in an orderly wind-down? Useful as a floor.
3. **Private market value / stock market value** — what would an informed acquirer pay? What multiples have similar businesses traded at?

Triangulate. If the methods diverge wildly, understand why before proceeding.

## Step 3: Margin of safety calculation

The margin of safety is the discount of price to value. Klarman wants it wide enough to absorb:
- Analytical error
- Bad luck
- Macro deterioration
- Time

Ask: what assumptions are baked into the value estimate? If those assumptions are wrong by 25%, is there still a margin? If not, it's not a Klarman investment.

## Step 4: Risk-first thinking

Risk is the probability and magnitude of permanent capital loss — not volatility. Address explicitly:

- **What could go wrong?** Enumerate 3–5 scenarios.
- **How much would be lost in each?** Quantify.
- **What is the probability-weighted downside?**
- **Are there hidden liabilities?** Off-balance-sheet, pending litigation, pension shortfalls, customer concentration, regulatory exposure.

A position that could lose 80% in a realistic scenario fails the test, even if base-case upside is large.

## Step 5: Catalysts

Klarman cares about catalysts because they shorten the time to value realization. Without a catalyst, value can sit unrecognized for years and compound poorly. Identify:

- **Hard catalysts** — known events: spin-off date, asset sale, liquidation, bankruptcy emergence, lawsuit resolution.
- **Soft catalysts** — improving fundamentals, activist involvement, sector rotation, management change.
- **No catalyst** — possible but requires deeper discount.

## Step 6: Why does this opportunity exist?

A real edge requires a real reason. The pricing inefficiency must be explainable:
- Forced selling (index changes, ratings downgrades, fund redemptions)
- Structural neglect (too small, too complex, too obscure)
- Recent disappointment (earnings miss, scandal, dividend cut)
- Sector or country aversion
- Spin-off dynamics (parent shareholders dumping the new entity)

If you can't articulate why the opportunity exists, suspect it doesn't.

## Step 7: Position sizing and portfolio context

Klarman holds cash when nothing meets the bar. A position size should reflect:
- Conviction in the margin of safety
- Quality of the catalyst
- Correlation to other positions
- Liquidity

## Output format

1. **Opportunity classification** — undervalued / special situation / distressed / liquidation / complex.
2. **Valuation triangulation** — at least two methods with explicit numbers.
3. **Margin of safety** — quantified, with stress test.
4. **Downside scenarios** — 3–5 ways to lose, with magnitudes.
5. **Catalysts** — what realizes value and when.
6. **Why this exists** — explicit explanation of the mispricing.
7. **Verdict** — invest, pass, or wait; with the dispositive factor.

## What to avoid

- Don't lean on a single valuation method.
- Don't ignore downside because base-case upside is exciting.
- Don't recommend without articulating why the opportunity exists — that's the most common way to be wrong.
- Don't treat volatility as risk. Permanent loss is risk.
- Don't anchor to recent price. Anchor to value.
