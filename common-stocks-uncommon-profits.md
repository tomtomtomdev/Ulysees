# Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits — Fisher Framework

Fisher's contribution: the most important determinants of long-term returns are qualitative, and finding them requires going beyond financial statements. Apply the fifteen points plus scuttlebutt method.

## Step 1: The fifteen points

Work through each. Many require qualitative judgment — say what's known, what's assumed, what's unknown.

1. **Products or services with sufficient market potential** to enable substantial growth for several years.
2. **Management determined to develop new products/services** to extend growth when current lines mature.
3. **Effectiveness of research and development** relative to size.
4. **Above-average sales organization** that converts product strength into revenue.
5. **Worthwhile profit margins** in absolute terms and improvement trajectory.
6. **What is being done to maintain or improve margins** — pricing power, cost discipline, operating leverage.
7. **Outstanding labor and personnel relations** — turnover, training, internal mobility.
8. **Outstanding executive relations** — depth of bench, succession, internal promotion vs. external hire pattern.
9. **Depth of management** — not a one-person company.
10. **Cost analysis and accounting controls** — does management know its own economics in detail?
11. **Industry-specific factors** — clues unique to the business that distinguish leaders from followers (real estate quality for retail, royalty structures for media, etc.).
12. **Short-range vs. long-range outlook on profits** — willing to sacrifice near-term for genuine long-term advantage.
13. **Future equity financings** that would dilute existing holders for growth — can the company self-fund?
14. **Management candor in good times and bad** — willing to discuss problems openly.
15. **Management of unquestionable integrity** — the single non-negotiable point.

A truly excellent investment scores well on the great majority of these. A few weak points are acceptable if compensated; integrity (#15) is not.

## Step 2: Scuttlebutt research

The fifteen points can't be answered from financials alone. Scuttlebutt is the practice of gathering opinions from people who actually know the company:
- **Customers** — would they switch, why or why not
- **Suppliers** — payment terms, relationship quality, who else they supply
- **Competitors** — what they fear and respect (or don't) about this company
- **Former employees** — culture, turnover reasons, management quality
- **Industry experts** — sell-side analysts, trade publications, conference takeaways

For the analysis, identify the scuttlebutt sources that would be most useful and what specific questions to ask. Where direct scuttlebutt isn't possible, surface the available proxies: Glassdoor reviews, customer NPS, churn rates, supplier concentration disclosures, industry forum sentiment.

## Step 3: Growth runway

The Fisher thesis depends on long compounding. Address:
- **Total addressable market** — large enough to support years of growth?
- **Penetration today** — early or late?
- **Competitive structure** — does the leader keep most of the gains, or commoditize?
- **Geographic expansion potential** — domestic saturation can be offset by international.

A great company in a saturated market is not the Fisher pattern.

## Step 4: Valuation — Fisher's view

Fisher was willing to pay up for genuine quality. But "pay up" is not "pay anything." Address:
- Current P/E relative to historical range for this stock
- P/E relative to other quality compounders
- Implied growth rate at current price — is it baked-in or still ahead?
- What scenarios justify the current price; what scenarios make it cheap

Fisher's preferred buying moments: temporary disappointment in a great business — earnings miss, transition year, sector rotation against it — when quality is on sale for cyclical reasons.

## Step 5: When to sell — Fisher's three reasons

Address each in advance:
1. **Mistake** — original analysis was wrong; the company doesn't have the qualities you thought.
2. **Company has deteriorated** — lost the qualities it once had (new management, lost moat, R&D dried up).
3. **A meaningfully better opportunity exists** — high bar; routine portfolio shuffling is not a Fisher reason.

Note: "the stock has gone up" is not on the list. Quality compounders are meant to be held.

## Output format

1. **Verdict** — uncommon-profits candidate / good but not Fisher-grade / fails Fisher test.
2. **Fifteen-point scorecard** — pass/concern/fail for each, with one-line evidence.
3. **Scuttlebutt plan** — sources to consult, key questions; or summary of available proxies.
4. **Growth runway** — TAM, penetration, competitive durability.
5. **Valuation context** — paying up vs. paying any price.
6. **Sell triggers** — what would change the verdict.
7. **Confidence level** — what's known vs. assumed.

## What to avoid

- Don't reduce Fisher to financials. The whole point is that qualitative factors dominate over long horizons.
- Don't assume good current results imply Fisher-grade quality. Look for durability of the qualities, not just recent outcomes.
- Don't pay any price. Fisher paid up; Fisher didn't pay anything.
- Don't fail to address integrity (#15). It is the single dealbreaker.
- Don't conflate growth with quality. Many growth companies fail Fisher's tests.
