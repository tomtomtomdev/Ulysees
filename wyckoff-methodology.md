# Wyckoff Methodology — Villahermosa Framework

The Wyckoff method analyzes markets by reading the interaction of supply and demand through price and volume, with the goal of identifying what the Composite Operator (the aggregate of large, informed players) is doing.

## Step 1: The three laws

Every Wyckoff analysis runs through the three laws:

**Law of Supply and Demand**
- When demand exceeds supply, price rises
- When supply exceeds demand, price falls
- This is the foundation; everything else is application

**Law of Cause and Effect**
- Time spent building a cause (accumulation or distribution range) produces a proportional effect (the subsequent trend)
- Wider, longer ranges = larger subsequent moves
- Quantified via point-and-figure counts of the trading range

**Law of Effort vs. Result**
- Effort is volume; result is price movement
- Heavy volume with little price progress = absorption (effort without result, meaning the move is being faded by opposing flow)
- Light volume with sharp price moves = exhaustion (result without effort, meaning the move is unlikely to continue)
- Mismatch between effort and result is the key signal

## Step 2: Identify the structure

A Wyckoff analysis requires the chart to be in one of four structures:

- **Accumulation** — sideways range after a downtrend, Composite Operator building a long position
- **Markup** — uptrend following accumulation
- **Distribution** — sideways range after an uptrend, Composite Operator unloading
- **Markdown** — downtrend following distribution

State which structure applies. Trends (markup/markdown) require different analysis than ranges (accumulation/distribution).

## Step 3: Accumulation schematic — phase by phase

If the structure is accumulation, identify the phase by events:

**Phase A — Stopping the prior downtrend**
- **PS (Preliminary Support)** — first significant buying after downtrend
- **SC (Selling Climax)** — capitulation: heavy volume, wide-spread bar, often a tail
- **AR (Automatic Rally)** — sharp bounce as supply dries up
- **ST (Secondary Test)** — re-test of SC low on lower volume; should hold

**Phase B — Building the cause**
- Trading range establishment
- Composite Operator accumulates through ups and downs of the range
- Volume contracts over time as supply is absorbed

**Phase C — The test**
- **Spring** — false breakdown below range support that quickly reverses. Volume on the spring tells you whether supply is genuine (heavy volume = real supply, possibly invalid spring) or exhausted (light volume = clean spring)
- May also see **shakeout** — a more violent spring
- This is the highest-conviction entry zone

**Phase D — Markup begins inside the range**
- **SOS (Sign of Strength)** — wide-spread, high-volume rally on the back of the spring
- **LPS (Last Point of Support)** — pullback to higher low; lower volume; clean continuation entry
- Range high is broken cleanly

**Phase E — Trend out of the range**
- Sustained markup
- Pullbacks find support; volume on advances exceeds volume on declines

## Step 4: Distribution schematic — phase by phase

Distribution mirrors accumulation with reversed roles:

**Phase A — Stopping the prior uptrend**
- **PSY (Preliminary Supply)** — first significant selling
- **BC (Buying Climax)** — euphoric high on heavy volume, wide bar
- **AR (Automatic Reaction)** — sharp decline as demand exhausts
- **ST (Secondary Test)** — re-test of BC on lower volume; should fail to make new high

**Phase B — Building the cause for the decline**
- Trading range; Composite Operator distributing

**Phase C — The test**
- **UTAD (Upthrust After Distribution)** — false breakout above range that fails
- High volume on the UTAD = real supply present and stepping in
- Highest-conviction short entry

**Phase D — Markdown begins inside the range**
- **SOW (Sign of Weakness)** — wide-spread, high-volume decline
- **LPSY (Last Point of Supply)** — weak rally to lower high; clean continuation short entry
- Range low breaks

**Phase E — Trend out of the range**
- Sustained markdown

## Step 5: Composite Operator inference

For the chart in question, infer what the Composite Operator is doing:
- Accumulating quietly (Phase B accumulation)? Testing for supply (Phase C spring)?
- Distributing into strength (Phase B distribution)? Trapping breakout buyers (UTAD)?
- Already markup or markdown — riding the trend?

Frame in terms of intent, not just pattern. Wyckoff's discipline is to think like the operator.

## Step 6: Effort vs. result diagnosis

At each significant bar, ask:
- Volume relative to recent bars
- Spread (range from high to low) relative to recent bars
- Close position within the bar (top third, middle, bottom third)
- Is the result (price progress) proportional to the effort (volume)?

Mismatches are the diagnostic moments — heavy volume with narrow range and close in the middle is absorption; light volume with wide range and strong close is no-supply (bullish) or no-demand (bearish), depending on context.

## Step 7: Point-and-figure cause count

Once accumulation or distribution is identified and a breakout occurs, count the cause:
- Use point-and-figure (P&F) on the trading range
- Each column of the P&F is a unit of cause
- Multiply column count by box size by reversal amount for a target estimate
- This gives a price target derived from the size of the cause, not arbitrary

## Output format

1. **Structure** — accumulation, markup, distribution, markdown.
2. **Phase** — A, B, C, D, or E with the events identified.
3. **Key events** — PS, SC, AR, ST, spring, SOS, LPS / PSY, BC, AR, ST, UTAD, SOW, LPSY as applicable.
4. **Three laws check** — supply/demand bias, cause built, effort vs. result mismatches.
5. **Composite Operator inference** — what the operator is doing.
6. **Cause count target** — P&F-derived target if applicable.
7. **Entry, stop, target** — trade construction based on phase and pattern.
8. **What would invalidate** — clean failure conditions.

## What to avoid

- Don't force Wyckoff phases onto charts that aren't clearly in a range. Trends should be analyzed as trends.
- Don't read springs without volume context. A spring on heavy volume that holds is bullish; a spring on heavy volume that breaks down is not.
- Don't use cause counts as precise targets. They're approximate.
- Don't apply Wyckoff to illiquid names where volume signals are noise.
- Don't assume the Composite Operator is always right or always present. Sometimes ranges are just ranges.
