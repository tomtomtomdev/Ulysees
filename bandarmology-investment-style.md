# Bandarmology — Investment Style Framework

Filbert's investment-style application of bandarmology: same flow detection principles as the short-term version, but applied to position trading on a multi-month or multi-quarter horizon. The thesis: large operators build positions over months, and an investor (not trader) can ride alongside if accumulation is detected early enough and fundamentals support holding.

## Step 1: Time-horizon framing

This framework is for holding periods of 3–18 months, not days. Adjust expectations accordingly:
- Broker accumulation patterns should span months, not weeks
- Drawdowns of 10–20% during accumulation are normal
- Exit triggers are more about phase change than short-term price moves

## Step 2: Long-window broker accumulation

Examine broker data over a longer lookback (3–12 months):

- **Cumulative net buying by broker** over the window — which brokers have built large net positions?
- **Persistence across phases** — top accumulators present through pullbacks as well as rallies indicate genuine position-building.
- **Accumulation through shakeouts** — does the same broker buy during sharp declines? That's high-conviction position-building.
- **Average accumulation price** — the cost basis of the top accumulators is a key reference for your own entry.
- **Foreign institutional flow** — foreign net buying over months is a particularly strong signal on emerging markets exchanges.

## Step 3: Combine with fundamental floor

Filbert's investment-style version explicitly combines bandar flow with fundamentals. Even if accumulation is detected, the fundamental floor must hold:
- Earnings stable or improving over the accumulation window
- Balance sheet not deteriorating
- No major business deterioration or governance event
- Valuation not in extreme bubble territory

The reasoning: large operators occasionally accumulate failing stocks for tactical reasons (corporate actions, distressed plays), but the typical retail-friendly bandar accumulation pattern aligns with a real business case.

## Step 4: Identify the accumulation phase

Map to the bandarmology / Wyckoff framework with patient-investor lens:

- **Phase A — Stopping the decline**: selling climax, automatic rally. Earliest entry but lowest confirmation.
- **Phase B — Building the cause**: long sideways range, broker accumulation visible, occasional shakeouts. Best entry zone for position investors.
- **Phase C — Test (spring or shakeout)**: false breakdown that fails, top accumulators continue buying. Highest-conviction entry.
- **Phase D — Markup begins**: clear breakout above range, broker concentration continues. Entry still acceptable but margin of safety reduced.
- **Phase E — Trend mature**: stay long with trailing stops; watch for distribution signs.

Entry confidence is highest in late B or C, when the cause is built but markup hasn't run far.

## Step 5: Position sizing for the long horizon

Larger positions justifiable if:
- Broker accumulation is strongly concentrated and sustained
- Fundamental floor is solid
- Valuation is reasonable
- Multiple time-window confirmations align (weekly and monthly broker data both supportive)

Reduce position size if:
- Broker concentration is mixed or weakening
- Fundamentals are merely acceptable, not strong
- The stock is in a high-correlation market environment (everything moving together obscures stock-specific flow)

## Step 6: Exit and management

Unlike short-term bandarmology trading, investment-style exits are slower and pattern-based:
- **Phase E distribution signs** — prior accumulators flipping to net sellers consistently
- **Buying climax** — vertical price action on extreme volume followed by automatic reaction
- **Fundamental deterioration** — earnings disappointment, balance sheet stress, governance event
- **Target reached** — pre-defined target based on accumulation cause and historical resistance

Trailing-stop discipline: move stop up under each significant pullback's low as the trend matures.

## Step 7: What this framework does not promise

Honest framing:
- Bandar accumulation is one input, not a guarantee
- Multi-month timeframes mean you'll endure drawdowns; that's the price of admission
- False breakouts and failed accumulations happen; the stop discipline matters
- Some "accumulating brokers" are passive flows or single-client trades, not directional bets
- This framework is more reliable on liquid mid-caps than illiquid micro-caps where broker concentration may simply reflect thin trading

## Output format

1. **Verdict** — investment-grade accumulation / suggestive but unconfirmed / no accumulation / distribution risk.
2. **Phase identification** — A, B, C, D, or E with evidence.
3. **Long-window broker concentration** — top accumulators over 3–12 months, persistence, avg price.
4. **Fundamental floor check** — earnings, balance sheet, governance, valuation.
5. **Entry zone** — price range supported by accumulation evidence.
6. **Stop and target** — phase-based, not arbitrary.
7. **Position sizing** — recommended approach for this conviction level.
8. **Sell triggers** — what would prompt exit.

## What to avoid

- Don't apply day-trader sensitivity to a position-trade framework. Patience and tolerance for noise are required.
- Don't ignore fundamentals because the flow looks strong. The investment-style framework requires both.
- Don't enter in late Phase D after a large move. Risk-reward deteriorates fast.
- Don't size based on conviction alone. Concentration, phase, fundamentals, and macro environment all moderate size.
- Don't assume liquidity. Many bandar plays are on names with thin float; size for the exit, not the entry.
