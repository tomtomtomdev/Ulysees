# Security Analysis — Graham & Dodd Framework

A more rigorous companion to The Intelligent Investor. Use when the situation demands a thorough security-by-security investigation rather than a screen.

## Step 1: Classify the security

Different security types get different tests. Identify which applies:
- **Senior security with fixed claim** — bond or preferred stock. Apply coverage and protection tests.
- **Common stock — investment grade** — apply quantitative tests, then qualitative.
- **Common stock — speculative** — note the speculative elements explicitly; do not apply investment-grade conclusions.

## Step 2: Earning power, not just earnings

The single most important concept. Earning power is the company's normalized capacity to generate profit, not last year's accounting figure.

Compute:
- **Average earnings** over 7–10 years (a full cycle).
- **Trend** — improving, stable, declining, erratic.
- **Adjusted earnings** — strip out non-recurring items, gains on sales, reserve manipulations, aggressive accounting.

Earning power = the figure a prudent analyst would expect on average going forward, given the historical record and any structural changes.

## Step 3: Quantitative tests for common stock

Run all of these:

- **Earnings stability** — no deficits over 10 years; ideally rising trend.
- **Return on invested capital** — sustained, well above cost of capital.
- **Dividend record** — long, uninterrupted history adds credibility.
- **Financial strength** — current ratio ≥ 2, long-term debt manageable relative to working capital and equity.
- **Price relative to earning power** — P/E on average earnings, not peak earnings.
- **Price relative to tangible assets** — net asset value per share as a floor.

## Step 4: Bond and preferred stock tests

For fixed-income securities, the central question is safety, not yield.

- **Times-interest-earned (fixed-charge coverage)** — for industrials, minimum 3× over 7 years; for utilities, 2×; for railroads, 2× (Graham's original thresholds — adjust by industry today, but the principle holds: thick coverage).
- **Stock value above bonds** — equity cushion well in excess of the bond's claim.
- **Size of enterprise** — small companies need higher coverage to compensate.
- **Indenture terms** — covenants, seniority, security.
- **Industry character** — cyclical industries need higher coverage than stable ones.

A bond that fails coverage is a speculation, not an investment, regardless of yield.

## Step 5: Qualitative factors (use with discipline)

Graham was wary of qualitative factors because they invite paying any price for a "great" story. Address briefly:
- Nature of the business
- Management quality and integrity (look for it in the numbers and disclosures, not the press)
- Competitive position
- Industry outlook

These can disqualify but should rarely justify a premium.

## Step 6: Valuation

Three approaches, used together:

1. **Earning power × multiplier** — earning power times a multiple appropriate to the stability and growth profile. Stable, growing companies get higher multiples; cyclical/declining get lower.
2. **Asset value** — net tangible assets per share. Especially relevant for asset-heavy businesses, distressed situations, or liquidation analysis.
3. **Comparative analysis** — versus peers on earnings, dividends, balance-sheet strength, and price.

Range, not a point estimate.

## Step 7: The margin between price and value

State explicitly. A security is only an investment when the margin is substantial.

## Output format

1. **Security classification** — what is being analyzed and under which framework.
2. **Earning power analysis** — historical earnings, adjustments, normalized figure.
3. **Quantitative scorecard** — financial strength, coverage, returns.
4. **Qualitative notes** — brief.
5. **Valuation range** — earning power method + asset method + peer comp.
6. **Margin of safety** — explicit percentage.
7. **Conclusion** — investment, speculation, or unsound, with the dispositive reasons.

## What to avoid

- Don't conflate earnings with earning power. Last year is a data point, not a verdict.
- Don't accept reported numbers without adjustment — look for accounting choices that flatter the figure.
- Don't recommend a bond on yield alone. Coverage and cushion come first.
- Don't dress speculation as investment by leaning on qualitative narrative.
